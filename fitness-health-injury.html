Fitness, Health & Injury

Training smart is more important than training hard. Here's what that means in practice.

Building your base

Before speed work, there's base work. This is the step most athletes skip, and it's the reason most athletes get hurt in their first season back. Whether you're returning after years away from sport or picking up athletics for the first time, your tendons, ligaments, and connective tissue need weeks of progressive loading before you add intensity to the picture.

Eight to twelve weeks of aerobic and strength work before you start sprinting or doing jump training will reduce injury risk significantly. That's not a long time. It's two to three months of feeling like you're not doing much, followed by a year of actually being able to train consistently.

Base work means: easy running or brisk walking, two strength sessions per week focusing on lower body compound movements, hip mobility, and progressive loading of the Achilles and calves through calf raises and heel drops. Boring in the short term. Foundational in the long term.

Load management

Training load is volume multiplied by intensity. You can increase one or the other in a given week, but not both. This is where most athletes go wrong: they add more sessions (volume) at the same time as they start doing faster work (intensity), and the body can't adapt quickly enough. Acute spikes in load are the primary mechanism behind overuse injuries.

A simple rule: if you increased intensity this week (added a sprint session, a track workout, a plyometric circuit), keep volume flat or reduce it. If you increased volume (an extra session, a longer run), keep intensity moderate. One new stimulus per week is enough. The body adapts in the recovery periods between sessions, not during the sessions themselves.

Track your sessions. Not obsessively, but enough to see patterns. Most athletes who get injured can look back at their training log and find the week where they did too much. Don't be that person. The log is also where you'll find the evidence of what works, which is equally valuable.

Common injuries and prevention

Hamstring strains

The most common acute injury in masters track athletes. The mechanism is almost always sprinting or bounding without an adequate warm-up. Prevention: 15-20 minute warm-up with progressive intensity, no cold max-effort sprinting, eccentric hamstring work (Nordic curls, Romanian deadlifts) as a regular part of strength training.

Achilles tendinopathy

Tendon stiffness increases with age, and the Achilles is loaded heavily in all sprint, jump, and hurdle events. Progressive loading (heel drops, calf raises), not rest alone, is the evidence-based treatment. Avoid sudden increases in training volume on hard surfaces.

Shin splints

Usually a load management issue: too much too soon, or a sudden change in surface (from grass to synthetic track, or vice versa). Address footwear, reduce load, and return gradually. If pain is located along the shin bone rather than in the muscle belly, see a physio to rule out stress reaction.

Plantar fasciitis

Morning pain on the heel or arch is the classic presentation. Morning mobility work (towel stretch, calf raises on a step) and load management are the primary tools. Supportive footwear in daily life helps. This is a long-term condition that responds slowly: plan for 3-6 months of consistent treatment.

When to see a physio

These are clear signals to stop training and get assessed:

  • Pain that persists beyond 48 hours after a session
  • Pain that changes your gait or technique during a session
  • Any new, sharp pain during exercise
  • Swelling, bruising, or a sensation of something "giving way"
  • Pain in the same location that keeps returning despite rest

Do not push through these. Training through pain that falls into any of these categories turns a two-week problem into a two-month problem. The athletes who stay healthy over long careers are the ones who act early and act consistently.

Working with QMA

QMA does not have a medical staff on site at training sessions or meets. What we do have is a community of experienced athletes and coaches who have navigated most of the common injuries that come with masters track and field. If you're dealing with something and not sure where to start, ask at training.

QMA can connect members with recommended sports physios and GPs who work regularly with masters athletes in Queensland. Contact us at secretary@qldmastersathletics.org.au if you need a referral.
