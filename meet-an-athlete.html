Member Spotlight

Jess
Chen

Jess Chen was the fastest girl in her year at school. Then university, a career in marketing, two kids, and fifteen years passed without her stepping on a track. She came back six months after ACL reconstruction surgery, expecting to feel humbled. She left her first QMA session completely hooked.

Two years on, she is running her fastest 100m times since she was seventeen.

The comeback was not linear. There were sessions where the knee flared and she drove home in tears. But something about QMA kept her turning up.

"Nobody here is annoyed if you're slow one week. Everyone's dealing with something. A dodgy knee, a busy work week, three kids who didn't sleep. You just show up and do what you can."

She had not expected to cry over a training time. When she ran her first sub-13 second 100m since school in 2025, she did.

Jess competes in the W35 100m, 200m, and long jump. Her goal for 2026 is to qualify for the Australian Masters Athletics Championships. And if she is honest with herself, to see what a serious off-season training block might do to that 100m time.

"I genuinely think my best is still in front of me. I'm stronger now than I was at twenty-two. I just have to figure out how to sleep enough."

Member Spotlight

Marcus
Thompson

Marcus Thompson spent thirty years as a police officer and never once considered himself an athlete. He was a rugby league man until his knees said otherwise. At forty-seven, on a dare, he turned up to his first QMA session with no throwing background. Eight years later, he holds two Queensland state records.

He turned up expecting to feel ridiculous. He felt ridiculous for about two sessions. Then he started to improve.

"The shot put made sense to me straight away. It's an explosive movement, it rewards strength, it's technical but in a way that a front rower can understand. I was terrible. Then I was less terrible. That's all the motivation you need."

Marcus now holds two Queensland state records in the M55 shot put and discus, and finished on the podium at the 2024 Australian Masters Athletics Championships.

He trains four mornings a week and is targeting the 2026 World Masters Athletics Championships in Gothenburg. He needs a qualifying standard before the end of the year.

"At fifty-five I'm training harder than I ever did at twenty-five. The difference is I actually enjoy it now. Nobody's telling you that you have to be here. You're here because you want to be."

Member Spotlight

Diane
Walsh

In 2011, Diane Walsh's GP told her at fifty-three that her blood pressure and blood sugar were heading in the wrong direction. He suggested she walk more. She started running instead. Within two years she had joined QMA. Twelve years on, she has competed at two World Masters Athletics Championships and holds nine Queensland age group records.

Running from Toowoomba means long drives to Brisbane meets, early starts, and logistics that don't get easier with age. There are mornings she is up at four to drive down for a 7am race.

"You have to want it." She pauses. "I want it."

Diane manages type 2 diabetes, which she credits athletics with keeping in check. She has competed at the World Masters Athletics Championships in Tampere (2022) and Budapest (2023), finishing second in her age group at Budapest behind a Finnish athlete she had been chasing for three years.

"That's what masters athletics is. You're not chasing prize money. You're not chasing a contract. You're chasing people who love the same thing you love, all over the world."

At sixty-seven, she is targeting the 2027 World Masters Championships and has designs on one more Queensland record in the 5000m before her next age group change.

Member Spotlight

Kevin
O'Brien

Kevin O'Brien played 214 games of AFL football before retiring at thirty-seven with two knee operations behind him. He missed the structure and stakes of competitive sport. A physio mentioned QMA. He discovered the 400m hurdles and, despite hitting every single hurdle in his first session, has never looked back.

"I'd never hurdled anything in my life. In football you run around people, not over them. Apparently knocking all of them over is normal in your first session. It is not."

Five years on, Kevin competes nationally in both the 400m hurdles and flat 400m in the M45 category. He narrowly missed a podium finish at the 2025 Australian Masters Athletics Championships, a result he describes as "the best motivation I've ever had for an off-season."

The knee still requires management. He trains around it, not through it, which took years to learn.

"Masters athletics taught me to be smarter about my body. In football you'd strap it and play. Here you have coaches who understand adult bodies and how they respond differently. That knowledge is worth a lot."

His goal for 2026 is a podium at nationals. He has already qualified for the championships.

Member Spotlight

Priya
Sharma

Priya Sharma competed as a gymnast in India from age six to twenty. Then she moved to Brisbane for an engineering role and spent fifteen years not competing in anything. A Facebook ad for QMA in 2023 caught her eye. Three years later, she holds two Queensland age group records in high jump.

The jump into athletics was not as unlikely as it sounds. The hip flexibility and body awareness she had trained for fourteen years transferred immediately.

"My first session, the coach asked if I'd jumped before. I said gymnastics. He said: that explains a lot."

Training around a demanding engineering role takes discipline she finds almost comically intense.

"I have a spreadsheet for my training. My husband finds it very funny. I find it necessary."

Her proudest moment was clearing 1.56m at the 2025 Queensland State Masters Championships, a height that would have been competitive in open women's high jump at her school. She was forty-two at the time.

She is now competing in triple jump, where her gymnastics background is even more directly useful, and is targeting a third Queensland record before the 2027 age group change.

"My gymnastics coach would be confused about what I'm doing. But I think she'd understand the mentality. You don't stop wanting to be better."
